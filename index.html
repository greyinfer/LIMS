SECTION 01 · USE CASES — TIER 1

Core Use Cases
Table Stakes for Any LIMS

These are non-negotiable. Any LIMS that cannot execute these reliably is not a LIMS — it's a database with a laboratory theme. These represent the foundational buying criteria.

Sample Management & Chain of Custody
CORE Foundational · All Industries
  • Sample registration with full metadata capture (source, type, condition, collector)
  • Barcode / RFID / QR-based labelling and scanning
  • Chain of custody — who handled what, when, and where
  • Sample splitting, aliquoting, and derivative tracking
  • Sample storage location management (freezer, shelf, rack, box, position)
  • Sample disposal and destruction authorisation with audit trail
  • Retention scheduling and automated expiry alerts
  • Sample login queues and batching for high-throughput labs
```
Workflow & Task Management
CORE Foundational · All Industries
  • Configurable test and assay workflows with defined steps and dependencies
  • Role-based work queue assignment — analyst, reviewer, approver
  • Real-time workflow status tracking and turnaround time monitoring
  • Multi-step approval chains with electronic co-sign and witness requirements
  • SOP linkage within workflow steps — surfaced contextually, not in a document library
  • Workflow branching based on result ranges (e.g., auto-escalate OOS)
  • Workload forecasting and analyst capacity visibility
  • Repeat and re-test authorisation workflows with original result preservation
Result Entry, Validation & Calculation
CORE Foundational · All Industries
  • Structured data entry with field-level validation rules and reference ranges
  • Built-in calculation engine — dilution factors, conversions, derived results
  • Automatic OOS / OOT / specification check with alert and hold
  • Instrument result import via direct integration or file parse
  • Manual override with mandatory justification and dual-sign
  • Units conversion and normalisation across assays
  • Partial result save and resume without data loss
  • Inter-laboratory data submission and external result import
Reporting & Certificate Generation
CORE Foundational · All Industries
  • Certificate of Analysis (CoA) — auto-populated, printable, e-signed
  • Test report generation with configurable templates per client/product
  • Batch release reports with full result summary and specification check
  • Standard statistical reports — mean, SD, CV, trending, control charts
  • Scheduled report delivery to internal and external stakeholders
  • Custom report builder for ad-hoc analysis without IT dependency
  • Export to PDF, Excel, LOINC, HL7 FHIR formats
  • Audit-ready study record compilation for regulatory review
Compliance, Audit Trail & E-Signature
CORE Regulated Industries · GxP
  • Immutable, timestamped audit trail on every data event — create, modify, delete
  • 21 CFR Part 11 and Annex 11-compliant electronic signatures
  • Role-based access control with full permission audit
  • ALCOA+ data integrity compliance by design (Attributable, Legible, Contemporaneous, Original, Accurate)
  • Password policy management, session timeout, and access revocation
  • Data backup, disaster recovery, and retention policy enforcement
  • Periodic system access review and orphan account detection
Instrument & Equipment Management
CORE All Lab Types
  • Instrument registration and status tracking (in-service, calibration, out-of-service)
  • Calibration scheduling with automated due-date alerts and overdue blocks
  • Preventive maintenance tracking and work-order generation
  • Instrument-to-result linkage — every result tagged to the equipment that generated it
  • Out-of-tolerance instrument flagging — automatic hold on pending results
  • Service record and repair history per instrument
  • Qualification documentation (IQ/OQ/PQ) repository
```

How LIMS Requirements Differ by Industry
One Platform Cannot Serve All Labs Equally

A diagnostics lab and a drug discovery lab share the LIMS label but have near-opposite priorities. Understanding these variations is essential for product positioning and feature prioritisation.

Pharmaceuticals
Highly regulated drug development and QC. Focus is on compliance, batch release, stability, and regulatory submission. Every data point must be defensible to FDA/EMA.
  • 21 CFR Part 11 / Annex 11
  • ICH Q guidelines (Q7, Q9, Q10)
  • GMP / GLP environment
  • Electronic Batch Record
  • Regulatory submission packages
Stability Study Management
ICH-protocol driven scheduling, automated pull lists, accelerated and real-time study arms, regression and shelf-life prediction with statistical confidence intervals.
Validated System Architecture
Computer System Validation (CSV) with full IQ/OQ/PQ documentation. Risk-based validation approach per GAMP 5. Change control with re-validation impact assessment.

Multi-tier approval workflow. Conditional release. Reject and rework authorisation. Integration with ERP batch status. Regulatory hold management.
OOS / OOT Investigation Management
Structured Phase 1 (lab investigation) and Phase 2 (full investigation) workflows with CAPA linkage, re-test authorisation, invalidation justification, and regulatory reportability assessment.
Biotechnology
Biologics development, cell and gene therapy, genomics, and protein science. High complexity samples, iterative R&D workflows, and emerging regulatory frameworks. Speed and flexibility matter as much as compliance.
  • Cell line tracking and genealogy
  • Biologics-specific stability
  • Sequence data management
  • BLA / MAA submission support
  • Cold chain integrity
Cell & Gene Therapy Tracking
Patient-specific sample genealogy. Autologous vs allogeneic tracking. Chain of identity from donor to patient dose. Lentiviral / AAV vector lot management.
NGS & Genomics Integration
Sequencing library prep tracking. Run-to-result traceability. Integration with Illumina DRAGEN, GATK pipelines. Multi-omics data linkage per sample.
Biobank & Biorepository
Long-term biospecimen storage management. Sample viability tracking. Consent-driven access controls. Automated pull lists for studies. Cryogenic storage integration.
ELN-First Research Workflows
Discovery-phase labs need flexible ELN with LIMS-grade tracking. Experiment-driven sample creation. Free-form observation capture alongside structured results. IP timestamping.
Diagnostics
Clinical and reference laboratories processing high volumes of patient samples. Turnaround time, HL7/FHIR integration, and critical value management are paramount. Volume can be 1,000–50,000 samples per day.
  • HL7 v2.x and FHIR integration
  • ISO 15189 / CLIA compliance
  • Critical value alerting
  • TAT (Turnaround Time) SLAs
  • Reflex testing logic
High-Throughput Sample Processing
Automated sample sorter integration (e.g., Roche MPA). Batch processing queues. Pre-analytical tracking (centrifuge, aliquoting). Tube-level tracking at scale.
Reflex & Reflex Testing Logic
Auto-triggered follow-on tests based on initial result (e.g., HIV screen reactive → confirmatory Western Blot). Rule-based reflexing without manual intervention.
Turnaround Time Dashboards
Real-time TAT monitoring by test, department, and priority. Escalation alerts for SLA breach. Bottleneck identification. Client-facing TAT reporting portal.
LIS Bi-directional Integration
Seamless HL7 ADT, ORM, ORU message exchange with hospital LIS and EMR systems. FHIR R4 result submission. Critical value push notification to clinical teams.
Clinical Research (CRO / Central Lab)
Multi-sponsor, multi-protocol environment. Subject-centric sample tracking with strict chain of identity. EDC integration and sponsor data delivery are core. Studies span years and multiple sites globally.
  • ICH E6 GCP compliance
  • Multi-sponsor data separation
  • EDC / CTMS integration
  • Global site coordination
  • Chain of identity (patient→sample)
Protocol-Driven Sample Management
Visit-level sample expectations defined by protocol. Kit management and pre-labelling for site kits. Expected vs received reconciliation. Protocol amendment management.
Subject Blind / Unblinding Controls
Randomisation code-linked sample management. Controlled unblinding workflows. Treatment arm-aware reference ranges. Patient-level data firewall between sponsor studies.
EDC Data Transfer
Automated result transmission to EDC (Medidata Rave, Oracle InForm, Veeva Vault). CDISC CDASH/SDTM mapping. Query response management. Database lock readiness tracking.
Multi-Site Global Coordination
Site-specific reference ranges by geography and demographics. Timezone-aware TAT tracking. Global vs local accreditation compliance. Multi-currency billing integration.
Manufacturing QC
Quality control labs embedded in production environments. Speed of release is a competitive and operational imperative. Integration with MES and ERP is non-negotiable. Real-time release testing is the frontier.
  • GMP batch record integration
  • MES / ERP real-time sync
  • In-process QC workflows
  • PAT / RTRT readiness
  • Rapid micro testing
In-Process Quality Control
Sampling triggers from MES at defined process steps. Go/no-go decision feedback to manufacturing floor. Hold/release flags propagated in real time. Parametric release pathways.
Raw Material Testing & Release
Incoming material testing queued from ERP goods receipt. Supplier CoA comparison and verification. Reduced testing programs for approved suppliers. Auto-release on passing result.
Environmental Monitoring
Scheduled environmental sampling plans per ISO 14644. Trend analysis for particulate and microbial excursions. Automated alert escalation. Cleanroom classification maintenance records.
SPC & Statistical Release
Real-time Shewhart control charts. Westgard multi-rule QC. Western Electric rules. cpk/ppk trending. Automated release when SPC criteria met within validated bounds.

Grant & Project Management
Samples and experiments tagged to grant IDs and project codes. PI-level visibility across group members. Resource consumption tracked against grant budget. Publication-ready data export.
Collaborative Research

SECTION 05 · MARKET ANALYSIS

Gaps & Limitations in Current LIMS
The Failure Map

Categorised by gap type with business impact rating. These are the failure modes that create market opportunity and justify next-generation platform investment.

Functional Gaps
Cross-Study Federation
Siloed study data with no federated query layer. Each study is an island. Labs cannot ask "across all stability studies in the last 3 years, what was the failure rate for assay X?" without a custom BI project. This is architecturally foundational and almost universally absent.
CRITICAL
Adaptive Workflows
Workflow engines designed for QC, not discovery. Sequential, rule-based workflows cannot handle the iterative, hypothesis-driven nature of R&D. Branching logic is rudimentary. No concept of a "learning" workflow that adapts based on prior run outcomes.
CRITICAL
Biologics & CGT
Small-molecule LIMS heritage fails on complex biologics. Cell and gene therapy requires patient-specific chain of identity, living cell tracking, passage number management, and virology testing workflows that legacy LIMS was never designed to handle.
HIGH
Multi-Tenancy
Global deployment requires true multi-tenancy with data sovereignty controls. Most platforms are single-instance deployments per site. CROs and global pharma operating across 20+ jurisdictions need region-aware data isolation with global aggregate views.
HIGH
Technology Gaps
AI Integration
Zero predictive intelligence in incumbent systems. Current LIMS is reactive — it records what happened. No platform offers credible predictive OOS models, adaptive scheduling intelligence, or AI-assisted root cause generation trained on lab-specific historical data.
CRITICAL
Instrument Connectivity
Every integration is a bespoke custom project. There is no universal instrument abstraction layer. HPLC, mass spec, and plate reader integrations are individually coded and break on firmware updates. A lab with 40 instrument types has 40 fragile middleware connections.
CRITICAL
UX Modernity
Enterprise UX from the early 2000s. Form-heavy, modal dialog-driven interfaces require 8–15 clicks for common tasks. No benchtop-optimised mobile view. Scientists spend 20–30% of bench time on data entry — a solvable problem that remains unsolved.
HIGH
Semantic Search
Keyword search across a decade of lab records is functionally useless. Scientists cannot find prior work by meaning. "Show me all experiments where we observed aggregation in antibody X at pH below 6" returns nothing without manual tagging. LLM-based search is transformative here.
HIGH
API Completeness
API-first in marketing; API-incomplete in reality. Core LIMS actions — sample login, result entry, batch release — remain UI-only in most platforms. External automation, robotic integration, and pipeline orchestration are blocked by incomplete API coverage.
HIGH
Operational & Business Gaps
Validation Tax
GMP validation requirements effectively freeze platform evolution. Any change, however minor, triggers CSV re-execution. Vendors cannot iterate their platform under GMP customer pressure. Labs get a system that was state-of-the-art in 2018 and cannot be safely updated.
CRITICAL
Implementation Cost
LIMS implementations routinely cost 3–5× the software licence. Median pharma LIMS implementation is $2–5M over 18–24 months. The implementation cost is a product design failure — it reflects how hard the software is to configure, not the complexity of the lab's requirements.
HIGH
Inspection Readiness
Compliance is a crisis response, not a continuous state. Labs spend 2–4 weeks before every FDA/EMA inspection compiling records that the LIMS already holds. No current platform provides a real-time inspection readiness score or auto-compiled data packages.
HIGH
Knowledge Retention
Institutional knowledge lives in senior scientists, not in systems. When experienced analysts leave, their understanding of method quirks, known instrument behaviours, and historical anomalies disappears. LIMS captures results but not context, rationale, or interpretation.
HIGH
SECTION 06 · USER RESEARCH

Pain Points by User Persona
Who Suffers, How, and Why It Matters

The same LIMS is experienced completely differently by a bench scientist, a lab manager, and a QA compliance lead. Designing for one persona at the expense of others is why LIMS systems are universally disliked by someone in every lab.

```
🔬
Bench Scientist / Analyst
PRIMARY USER · 6–8 HRS/DAY IN SYSTEM
Data Entry Theatre Re-entering results that the instrument already has electronically. Transcription takes 30–60 min/day and introduces error risk. The system is slower than paper for execution tasks.
"I just ran a 96-well plate. The instrument saved it. Now I have to type every number in again."
Context Switching Overhead SOP is in a document management system. Protocol is in LIMS. Notes are in a paper notebook. Instrument is talking to middleware. None of these are connected. Every task requires 4 open browser tabs.
No Intelligent Assistance When an anomalous result appears, the system flags OOS but provides no context — no history of similar flags, no reagent lot cross-reference, no analyst comparison. Scientists investigate blindly.
"It tells me it's out of spec. It doesn't tell me if this has happened before or why."
Mobile Experience Failure Benchtop work requires gloves, limited desk space, and eyes on the sample. The LIMS requires a full keyboard, screen-sized UI, and a wired connection. Voice entry and tablet-first design are absent.
📊
Lab Manager / Operations Lead
OPERATIONAL OWNER · SCHEDULING, QC, CAPACITY
Zero Visibility Across the Lab Floor No real-time dashboard showing what is in-progress, what is late, where the bottlenecks are, and which instruments are tied up. Status is obtained by walking the floor or calling analysts.
"I genuinely don't know if we're on track to release the batch today without asking three people."
Instrument Scheduling is Manual Instrument booking is done via Outlook calendar or a whiteboard. When instruments break, there is no way to automatically reassign pending work to available capacity. Rescheduling is entirely manual.
Report Generation is a Project Monthly KPI reports require exporting LIMS data to Excel, reformatting, calculating metrics, and building charts. A task that should auto-generate takes 4–8 hours every month.
Staff Training Complexity LIMS onboarding for new analysts is weeks-long because the system has no contextual guidance. Every task requires referencing an external training manual. Turnover is expensive in LIMS-heavy labs.
"We have a 40-page SOP just for how to use the LIMS. That is a UX problem."
⚖️
QA / Compliance Lead
COMPLIANCE OWNER · AUDIT READINESS, CAPA, GxP
Inspection Preparation Crisis Every regulatory inspection triggers a 2–4 week emergency to compile study records, verify audit trail completeness, check e-signature coverage, and close open CAPAs. This should be a real-time state, not a crisis response.
"We found three unsigned records the night before an FDA inspection. That was a bad night."
CAPA Linkage is Manual OOS investigation in LIMS is disconnected from the CAPA in the QMS. QA manually cross-references systems to track whether corrective actions were completed. Nothing is automated or linked.
Data Integrity Verification is Reactive Audit trail review happens after the fact during investigations. There is no proactive data integrity monitoring — no alerts for suspicious patterns like results entered at unusual hours, backdated entries, or duplicate submissions.
"ALCOA+ is a principle. The system doesn't actually enforce it proactively."
Change Control Bottleneck Every system change requires validation documentation. Simple configuration changes take months. QA is the gatekeeper for every update, making the system an obstacle to laboratory improvement.
```

🧱
Composable Over Monolithic
Functionality is delivered as validated building blocks, not a monolithic application. Labs assemble their LIMS without custom development. Each block is independently certified, versioned, and replaceable. Validation is inherited from the block, not re-executed per deployment. This reduces implementation time from 18 months to 6 weeks.
🧠
AI-Native, Not AI-Augmented
AI is not a feature layer bolted onto a workflow engine. The AI core is trained on each organisation's own data and is the primary interface for investigation, search, and insight generation. The workflow engine executes what the AI and scientist agree on. Intelligence is structural, not optional.
🔗
Integration-First Architecture
Every capability is API-complete from day one. No functionality exists only in the UI. The platform is designed to be a node in an enterprise data fabric — consuming from instruments, MES, ERP, and clinical systems, and producing for regulatory, BI, and AI analysis. Integration is not an afterthought — it's the product.
⚖️
Compliance as Infrastructure
The compliance layer is not a feature that QA turns on. Audit trail, e-signature, data integrity, and inspection readiness are foundational infrastructure that exists before any workflow is configured. You cannot turn off ALCOA+ compliance any more than you can turn off the database.
🌐
Federated Multi-Site Intelligence
Global organisations get aggregate intelligence without surrendering data sovereignty. Each site's data is governed locally. A global AI layer trained on federated, anonymised signals surfaces cross-site patterns — method failure correlations, supply chain risks, instrument drift trends — that no single-site LIMS can see.
🎯
Persona-Centred Design
Scientist, lab manager, and QA lead are served by fundamentally different interfaces optimised for their context. The scientist gets a benchtop-optimised, voice-capable minimal UI. The manager gets a real-time operations dashboard. The QA lead gets a compliance posture view. One system, three experiences, all from the same data.
The lab is the most data-rich, compliance-critical environment in the enterprise — and the most poorly served by its software.

The next generation of LIMS will not be won by the vendor with the most features or the deepest compliance capability. It will be won by the platform that makes laboratory intelligence effortless — where AI surfaces what matters, compliance is invisible because it's automatic, and scientists spend their time on science rather than software. The architecture is achievable today. The market gap is real. The timing is right.
